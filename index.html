Money Didn't Change Me. My Relationship With It Did.

Money Mindset · intentionalthrive.com/blog/article-1

Facebook
LinkedIn
Reel Script
Carousel
There was a pattern in how I handled money that I didn't notice until I started earning more - and it didn't go away. More income. Same cycle. Just bigger numbers. It took me years to realize: the problem wasn't money. It was the story I carried about money - the invisible script running behind every financial decision I made. And a lot of us inherited versions of the same script: "As long as there's enough for today, we're fine." "Don't be greedy." "Money is just luck." These aren't bad beliefs. They're survival wisdom from people who loved us. But there's a difference between surviving and designing your financial life. That shift - from reacting to money to being intentional about it - is what Intentional Thrive is about. Not just strategies. Not just products. The story underneath. That's where it starts. If this resonates, you're in the right place. (Full article now live - link in bio)
Early in my career, I assumed that more income would solve everything. It didn't. It just made the same patterns more visible. I was earning. I was busy. I was helping clients protect their families and plan their futures. And yet my own relationship with money was still running on an old, inherited script - one I hadn't examined or intentionally rewritten. The term for this is a money story: the beliefs and automatic responses around money that most of us formed before the age of ten. Here's what I've observed after years in financial services: strategy without mindset just amplifies existing patterns. The most effective financial planning I've seen doesn't start with products. It starts with a conversation about what money means to the person sitting across from you. That's the premise of Intentional Thrive - a space for Filipino professionals who are ready to examine that story and design something better. (First article now live - link in comments)

I had only changed my income. And those are two very different things. Your money story is the invisible script running underneath every financial decision you make. Most of us inherited ours before we were ten years old. And until we examine it - no amount of income, investment, or insurance will feel like enough. That's what Intentional Thrive is about. The Filipino Professional's Guide to Not Starting Over at 40

Wealth in Practice · intentionalthrive.com/blog/article-2

Facebook
LinkedIn
Reel Script
Carousel
Honest question - answer it truthfully for yourself: If your income stopped tomorrow, how long could you survive? One month? Three? Less? This isn't a judgment. It's a question most of us avoid because the answer isn't comfortable. This is the most common financial gap I see across all income levels - not a lack of earning, but a lack of structure. There are three things every Filipino professional needs in place before 40. Not because 40 is a deadline, but because the cost of waiting is compounding quietly in the background. I wrote about all three this week. Full article at intentionalthrive.com (Link in comments)
The most common financial gap I see among professionals isn't income. It's the distance between what they earn and what they've built. High salary. Thin safety net. No investment vehicle working quietly in the background. This isn't a discipline problem. It's a design problem. After years in financial services and hundreds of client conversations, I've distilled it to three layers every professional should build before life forces the conversation. Not a pitch. Not a product comparison. Just a clear, honest framework for where to start. Link in comments. (If you know someone who could use this, please share it forward.)
HOOK: "If your income stopped today - how long could you survive?" VOICE-OVER (Taglish): Most professionals I know can't answer that question comfortably. Not because they're not earning. They are. But there's a difference between income and wealth. Income is the stream. Wealth is the reservoir you build from it. There are three things every Filipino professional needs in place before 40. One: protection for your income - your ability to earn is your biggest financial asset. Two: an emergency fund that actually exists - real, separate, untouched. Three: something that grows without you - quietly compounding even when you're not watching. You don't need to have all three perfectly in place today. But you need to start. END CARD: Full article at intentionalthrive.com
SLIDE 1: The 3-Layer Financial Foundation Every Filipino Professional Needs - Venus | Intentional Thrive SLIDE 2: Layer 1 - Protect Your Income. Your greatest financial asset is not your savings. It's your ability to earn. SLIDE 3: What income protection looks like: Life insurance (the right kind, structured for your life stage). Coverage if you can't work. A plan that doesn't collapse when something unexpected happens. SLIDE 4: Layer 2 - Emergency Fund. 3-6 months of actual expenses. Liquid. Separate. Untouched. Not a credit card. A real fund. SLIDE 5: Layer 3 - Something That Grows Without You. An investment vehicle that compounds over time. Consistency matters more than the amount. SLIDE 6: The honest truth: Most Filipino professionals have none of these fully in place. Not because they can't. Because no one walked them through it clearly, without the sales pressure. What Life Insurance Actually Is

Wealth in Practice · CTA: DM COVERAGE · intentionalthrive.com/blog/article-4

Facebook
LinkedIn
Reel Script
Carousel
I have clients who've held their insurance for almost 5 years - and only recently realized they didn't fully understand what their policy actually covered. They weren't careless. They weren't uninformed. It just wasn't explained properly. That's the biggest problem in our industry: we sell coverage without really explaining what's being bought. Insurance isn't just a death benefit. It's a mechanism for protecting your financial plan when the unexpected happens. The question I ask every client before recommending anything: "What are you protecting?" When you can answer that clearly, the right structure becomes almost obvious. If you want to review what you have - or start from scratch - I'm here for that. No pressure, no quota. Just clarity. DM me or comment "COVERAGE"
After years in financial services, here's the thing I'd change about how our industry communicates: We lead with fear or obligation. Almost never with understanding. "What if you die?" is not the right opening question. The right question is: "What are you protecting?" When a professional can answer that clearly - the mortgage, the children's education, an aging parent, an income their household depends on - the right financial structure becomes almost self-evident. I wrote an article this week that tries to reframe how life insurance is explained - not as a sales tool, but as a planning tool. Not a pitch. Just the clearer version of a conversation this industry hasn't had enough of. Link in comments - share it if you think someone needs to read it.
HOOK: "Nobody explains insurance like this." VOICE-OVER (Taglish): Most people buy life insurance because a friend asked them to. Most people who don't have it think they'll get to it later. Both groups are working with incomplete information. Here's the version nobody gave you: Insurance isn't a bet against your life. It's a mechanism for protecting your financial plan from the moments that would otherwise destroy it. It answers one question: if something happened to you today - what happens to everything you're supporting? The mortgage. The children. The parents. The plan. Insurance is the answer you put in place before you need it. Because by the time you need it, it's too late. END CARD: DM 'COVERAGE' to start. | intentionalthrive.com
SLIDE 1: What Your Policy Actually Covers vs. What You Think It Covers - Venus | Intentional Thrive SLIDE 2: What most people think insurance is: A payout when you die. That's the most basic version. There's a lot more. SLIDE 3: What it can actually cover: Income replacement. Critical illness (cancer, stroke, heart attack). Education fund. Retirement component. Estate and legacy planning. SLIDE 4: The question your agent should have asked you: "What are you protecting?" Not what you can afford. What YOU are protecting. SLIDE 5: Term vs. Permanent (the honest version): Term = Maximum protection, lower cost, for a specific period. Permanent (VUL) = Lifelong coverage plus investment component. Neither is wrong. The wrong one is whichever you chose without understanding it. SLIDE 6: If any of this is new information: You didn't fail. You weren't told. The industry has a communication problem.
